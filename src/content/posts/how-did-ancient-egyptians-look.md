## A Face From 4,500 Years Ago

In 2025, scientists did something remarkable: they sequenced the most complete ancient Egyptian genome ever found, taken from a man buried in a sealed ceramic pot in the Egyptian village of Nuwayrat during the third millennium BCE. Using DNA extracted from one of his teeth, researchers worked with forensic specialists to produce a three-dimensional likeness of his face. The result — a man with dark features and brown skin — put a human face on one of history's most persistently contested questions: what did the ancient Egyptians actually look like?

It turns out the answer is both simpler and more layered than internet arguments tend to allow.

## Skin Tone: The Evidence and the Debate

Ancient Egypt was not a monolith. Spanning more than three thousand years — from the unification under Pharaoh Menes around 3100 BCE to the fall of Cleopatra's kingdom in 30 BCE — the civilization absorbed migrants, traders, and conquerors from across the Mediterranean, sub-Saharan Africa, and the Near East. Expecting a uniform appearance across that span is like expecting all Romans to share one face.

What the art record does show is a consistent visual convention: tomb paintings and temple reliefs typically depicted men with reddish-brown skin and women with lighter, yellowish tones. Historians caution against reading this literally; it was an artistic code, not portraiture. Nevertheless, the pigment choices point toward a population that was generally darker-skinned than northern Europeans. The broad scholarly consensus today describes the typical ancient Egyptian as having medium to dark-brown skin, similar in range to modern Egyptians and neighboring North African and Middle Eastern populations.

A landmark study published in the journal *Nature* in 2025, analyzing the whole genome of an Old Kingdom Egyptian, found ancestry patterns consistent with populations of northeastern Africa, with meaningful genetic ties to ancient peoples of the Levant and the Nile Valley further south. The authors were careful to note that a single sample cannot represent all of ancient Egypt, and that the population almost certainly shifted as the civilization interacted with neighboring cultures across the millennia.

## Hair, Eyes, and the Body

Dark hair and dark eyes appear to have been the norm among the native population — consistent with what mummies that retain their original hair reveal. Several mummies have been found with naturally dark, wavy, or straight hair. Some display reddish or lighter tones, but scholars generally attribute this to the chemical effects of the mummification process rather than natural coloring in life.

In terms of stature, ancient Egyptians were generally shorter than modern humans. Analysis of skeletal remains suggests women averaged roughly around 157–160 cm and men somewhat taller, though estimates vary by era and social class — better-nourished elite individuals tended to be taller. Faces depicted in sculpture and painting — from the serene idealism of the Old Kingdom to the more naturalistic portraits of the New Kingdom — show almond-shaped eyes, strong cheekbones, and a range of features consistent with a northeastern African population. Three-dimensional forensic reconstructions of mummies produced faces that researchers described as consistent with North African ancestry — people who would not look out of place in modern Egypt, Ethiopia, or the broader Mediterranean world.

## Fashion: Linen in the Desert Heat

The Egyptian climate demanded practicality. The dominant fabric was linen — made from flax grown along the Nile — prized for its breathability in scorching temperatures. Men typically wore simple white kilts, while women favored long, close-fitting sheath dresses, often with wide shoulder straps. White dominated for most of Egyptian history, though dyes were used for festive or ceremonial garments. Wealthier Egyptians wore finer, more translucent linen; laborers wore coarser weaves or worked with little clothing at all.

Sandals made from papyrus or leather were worn by those who could afford them; many ordinary Egyptians went barefoot. Jewelry — broad collars, earrings, bracelets, amulets — was prized across social classes, with materials ranging from gold and lapis lazuli for royalty to glazed faience ceramic for commoners.

## Makeup: Beauty, Medicine, and Magic

One of the most distinctive aspects of ancient Egyptian appearance was the universal use of cosmetics — by both men and women, across all social classes. The iconic black eye paint, kohl, was made primarily from galena (lead sulfide) or stibnite. Green eye paint was produced from malachite, a copper compound. These were not merely decorative: Egyptians believed thick lines of kohl reduced glare from the desert sun, and researchers have suggested lead-based compounds may have had some antibacterial properties, potentially helping ward off eye infections in a dusty environment. Ancient Egyptians also held that the makeup carried magical healing power — a belief documented across many surviving texts and tomb inscriptions.

Red ochre served as lip and cheek color. Perfumed oils and ointments were rubbed into the skin for both ritual and practical purposes, counteracting the drying desert climate. Shaving was common: many men, especially priests and officials, kept their heads and faces clean-shaven and wore elaborate wigs — made from human hair or plant fibers — for formal occasions. The ceremonial beards seen on pharaohs in sculpture were generally artificial, strapped-on pieces rather than real facial hair.

## What DNA Is Finally Telling Us

For decades, questions about ancient Egyptian ancestry were clouded by poor DNA preservation in hot, arid burial environments. The 2025 breakthrough published in *Nature* — sequencing a complete Old Kingdom genome — marked a turning point. The individual, buried around 4,500 years ago, showed ancestry most closely aligned with ancient northeastern African and Levantine populations, with some sub-Saharan African genetic input as well.

This is broadly consistent with earlier, less complete genetic studies conducted on later-period mummies. Researchers stress that one genome does not define a civilization that lasted three millennia; Egypt's population varied by region, era, and social class. The scientific debate is genuinely not fully settled. But the accumulated evidence increasingly supports a picture of a population that was distinctly African in origin, with long-standing ties to the Nile Valley, while absorbing outside influence over time — a population that would look familiar to anyone who has walked through modern Cairo or Aswan.

## Why It Still Matters

The question of what ancient Egyptians looked like has been freighted with modern identity politics for centuries — claimed at various moments by European, sub-Saharan African, and Middle Eastern perspectives, each with its own agenda. The honest answer is that Egypt was a crossroads civilization whose population was diverse across time and geography. None of those modern claims captures the whole picture.

What matters more is that we are finally developing scientific tools — ancient DNA sequencing, 3D forensic reconstruction, isotope analysis of bones and teeth — that can move the conversation from ideology toward evidence. The face of the man from Nuwayrat, reconstructed from 4,500-year-old DNA, is not a trophy for any contemporary identity claim. He is simply an ancient Egyptian — and by all the evidence we have, he looks like a person who might walk the streets of the Nile Valley today.
