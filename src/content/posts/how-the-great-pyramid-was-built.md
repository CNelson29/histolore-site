## The Question That Won't Go Away

The Great Pyramid of Giza has stood for roughly 4,500 years. Built for the pharaoh Khufu around 2560 BCE, it was the tallest human-made structure on Earth for nearly four millennia. Its scale is staggering: about 2.3 million blocks of stone, most weighing several tons, assembled with astonishing precision.

That scale is exactly why myths persist. Surely, people ask, ancient Egyptians couldn't have done this alone? The evidence says they could — and did. The real story is more impressive than any myth.

## Who Actually Built It

For a long time, popular culture imagined enslaved masses driven by whips. Archaeology tells a different story. Excavations near the pyramids uncovered a workers' village, complete with bakeries, breweries and evidence of a well-fed, organized workforce. The builders were skilled laborers and rotating crews of conscripted workers — not slaves — housed, fed and cared for by the state.

Workers who died on the job were buried in tombs near the pyramid, an honor that would not have been given to slaves. Some graffiti left by work gangs even preserves their team names.

## Moving the Stones

The biggest puzzle is how such heavy blocks were moved. The most widely accepted answer is ramps combined with sledges. Blocks were dragged on wooden sledges, and a discovery at an ancient alabaster quarry showed a ramp system with post holes — suggesting ropes and posts were used to help haul loads up inclines.

There's also a clever detail: a wall painting shows workers pouring water in front of a sledge. Modern experiments confirm that wetting sand reduces friction dramatically, making it far easier to drag heavy loads. The Egyptians appear to have understood this.

## Precision Without Modern Tools

The pyramid's base is level to within a few centimeters across its entire footprint, and it is aligned to the cardinal directions with remarkable accuracy. The Egyptians achieved this using simple but effective tools: plumb bobs, set squares, sighting rods and the stars for orientation.

To level the ground, they likely used water channels — water always finds its own level — as a reference. None of it required technology beyond what we know they had; it required patience, skill and organization on an enormous scale.

## How Long and How Many

Ancient sources and modern estimates suggest the project took around 20 years and employed tens of thousands of workers in rotating crews, not all at once. Supporting them demanded a vast supply chain of food, tools and logistics — arguably as impressive as the engineering itself.

## Why It Still Matters

The Great Pyramid endures as a reminder of what organized human effort can achieve without modern machines. Reaching for aliens or lost technology actually sells the ancient Egyptians short. Their real achievement was mastering logistics, labor and simple physics at a scale the world had never seen — and would not see again for a very long time.
